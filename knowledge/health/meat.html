# The Worth of Epidemiology Studies

Epidemiology = branch of medicine which deals with the incidence, distribution, and possible control of diseases and other factors relating to health. In nutrition, epidemiology studies are observational studies. These come with at least three problems:

  • Subjects are asked to fill out Food Frequency Questionnaires (FFQs) about their diet. The International Dietary Data Expansion Project lists as one of FFQs' weaknesses that "usual portion size questions are prone to measurement error". In some studies like this one, subjects were asked to list their average weekly intake of e.g. bananas, brokkoli, and chicken for the last year. It's likely that participants are not able to exactly recount their food intake, leading to erroneous data.

  • The Healthy user bias: vegeterians tend to have a healthier lifestyle beyond nutrition, e.g. working out more often, not smoking. Good studies need to make sure that the healthy user bias is addressed.

  • Measured relative risks are near insignificant: Outside nutrition, you'd need to show at least a 100% increase in mortality risk for your findings to be practically significant. A study about the mortality risk increase of smoking showed a 1000%-3000% risk increase. A study about whether meat is carcenogenic found an 18% increase.

# Why you should eat vegetables with meat

Processed meat is supposed to be carcenogenic, and the following processes are supposed to be behind it:

  • N-nitroso compounds
  • Heme iron
  • TMAO
  • Neu5GC
  • Heterocyclic amines (formed from cooking)

Vegetables reduce levels of N-nitroso compounds and heme iron cruciferous vegetables (e.g. Arugula, Bok choy, Broccoli, Brussels sprouts, Cabbage, Cauliflower, and Collard greens) reduce levels reduce heterocyclic amines.
